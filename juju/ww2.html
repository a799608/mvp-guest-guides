🔧 Section 1: Anton Drexler

  • German locksmith from Munich, born 1884; working-class metalworker turned politician
  • Founded the German Workers' Party (DAP) in January 1919, met in beer halls
  • Wrote pamphlet "My Political Awakening" laying out his ideas
  • Adolf Hitler joined in September 1919 after being sent to spy on a meeting
  • Renamed the NSDAP (Nazi Party) in February 1920
  • Hitler muscled Drexler out and took over in 1921; Drexler given empty "Honorary President" title
  • Without Drexler, no Nazi Party for Hitler to take over; died 1942

📜 Section 2: Nazi Ideology

  • Hitler and the Nazi Party took power in 1933 after WWI loss left Germany broke and humiliated
  • Core beliefs: extreme nationalism, fake "Aryan" master race, and anti-Semitism (hatred of Jews)
  • Built a totalitarian state — one party, no free press, controlled by propaganda
  • Indoctrinated kids via Hitler Youth; held book burnings to destroy banned ideas
  • Nuremberg Laws (1935) stripped Jewish Germans of citizenship and rights
  • Pushed militarism and "Lebensraum" (living space) — excuse to conquer neighbors
  • Persecution of Jews, Roma, disabled, LGBTQ, and opponents escalated into the Holocaust (6 million Jews murdered)

⚡ Section 3: Three Key Nazi Events

🎯 1. Beer Hall Putsch (1923)

  • "Putsch" = sudden attempt to overthrow government; Nov 8, 1923 in Munich's Bürgerbräukeller
  • Failed march through Munich; police killed 16 Nazis, Hitler arrested
  • Hitler served only 9 months in Landsberg Prison; wrote "Mein Kampf" (My Struggle)
  • Lesson learned: don't seize power by force — get elected instead

🔪 2. Night of the Long Knives (1934)

  • June 30, 1934: Hitler purged rival Ernst Röhm and the SA ("Brownshirts")
  • SS (led by Heinrich Himmler) executed 85–200+ rivals and old enemies
  • SA became powerless; SS rose to most-feared force in Nazi Germany
  • August 1934: Hitler became "Führer" — combined Chancellor + President = absolute power

💔 3. Night of Broken Glass / Kristallnacht (1938)

  • "Kristallnacht" = "Crystal Night" — named for streets covered in broken glass
  • Nov 9–10, 1938: coordinated pogrom against Jews in Germany and Austria
  • 267 synagogues burned, 7,500 businesses destroyed, 91 killed, ~30,000 sent to concentration camps
  • Widely seen as the start of the Holocaust — persecution turned to open state violence

⚫ Section 4: Heinrich Himmler & the SS

  • Heinrich Himmler: small, bespectacled ex-chicken farmer; joined Nazi Party in 1923
  • Hitler made him head of the SS (Schutzstaffel = "Protection Squadron") in 1929
  • After Night of the Long Knives (1934), SS became the most powerful force in Nazi Germany
  • SS ran the Gestapo (secret police), concentration camps, and death camps
  • Himmler organized the "Final Solution" — systematic murder of Europe's Jews
  • Holocaust killed ~6 million Jews plus millions of Roma, disabled, POWs, opponents
  • Largest death camp: Auschwitz (occupied Poland), 1+ million murdered
  • Captured by British May 1945; killed himself with a cyanide pill before trial

🏭 Section 5: Total War

  • "Total war" = a nation mobilizes ALL resources (economy, civilians, science) for the war effort
  • Line between soldier and civilian disappears; civilians and infrastructure became targets
  • US factories retooled for tanks/planes — Roosevelt called America the "Arsenal of Democracy"
  • Home front: rationing, victory gardens, war bonds, scrap drives; "Rosie the Riveter" symbolized women in factories
  • Strategic bombing of cities by both sides: London, Coventry, Hamburg, Dresden, Tokyo, Hiroshima, Nagasaki
  • Wartime science: radar, jet engines, early computers (Bletchley Park's Bombe cracked Enigma), atomic bomb
  • Total deaths: 70–85 million (~3% of world population); majority were civilians

⚡ Section 6: Blitzkrieg — Lightning War

  • Blitzkrieg = "lightning war" — Germany's fast, combined-forces strategy built on speed and surprise
  • Luftwaffe (air force) sent Stuka dive-bombers first to smash roads and defenses
  • Panzer tanks punched through enemy lines; motorized infantry followed in trucks
  • First used on Sept 1, 1939 against Poland (fell in 5 weeks) — start of WW2
  • 1940: rolled over Denmark, Norway, Netherlands, Belgium, Luxembourg, then France in just 6 weeks
  • Operation Barbarossa (June 1941): blitzkrieg invasion of the Soviet Union
  • Failed in USSR — too vast, supply lines stretched, brutal Russian winter froze tanks and men

🛩️ Section 7: Battle of Britain

  • Summer 1940: France had fallen; Hitler planned Operation Sea Lion to invade Britain
  • Germany first had to destroy the Royal Air Force (RAF) to control the skies
  • Luftwaffe attacked British airfields; RAF fought back with Spitfires and Hurricanes
  • Britain's secret weapon: radar chain along the coast warned of incoming attacks
  • Sept 1940: Hitler switched to bombing London — "the Blitz" — letting RAF airfields recover
  • October 1940: Hitler called off Sea Lion — first major German defeat of the war
  • Churchill honored the RAF pilots: "so much owed by so many to so few" (~544 RAF pilots died)

🤝 Section 8: The Big Three: Allied Leaders

  • "The Big Three" = leaders of UK, USA, USSR — different governments, united vs. Axis
  • Their alliance was the key political reason the Allies won WW2

🇬🇧 Winston Churchill (UK)

  • Prime Minister of Britain starting May 1940 through 1945
  • Famous defiant speeches: "blood, sweat and tears," "we shall fight on the beaches"
  • Refused to surrender during the Battle of Britain when UK stood alone
  • Returned as PM 1951–1955

🇺🇸 Franklin D. Roosevelt (USA)

  • FDR = 32nd US President, only one ever elected four times
  • Had polio — ran the country largely from a wheelchair
  • Created Lend-Lease (1941) to ship arms to UK and USSR before US entered war
  • Died April 12, 1945, weeks before V-E Day; Harry Truman took over

🇷🇺 Joseph Stalin (USSR)

  • Communist dictator of the Soviet Union from 1924–1953
  • Signed non-aggression pact with Hitler 1939; betrayed by Operation Barbarossa in 1941
  • USSR suffered ~27 million deaths — most of any country
  • Brutal authoritarian who purged millions, but Red Army crushed Germany on Eastern Front

⚓ Section 10: Pearl Harbor

  • December 7, 1941 — Japan’s surprise attack on US Pacific Fleet at Pearl Harbor, Oahu, Hawaii

  • US aircraft carriers were at sea — escaped (huge Japanese mistake)
  • FDR called it "a date which will live in infamy"; Congress declared war Dec 8

🚶 Section 11: Bataan Death March

  • Hours after Pearl Harbor, Japan also attacked the Philippines (US territory)
  • Gen. Douglas MacArthur retreated to the Bataan Peninsula on Luzon; no Navy help coming
  • FDR ordered MacArthur to Australia — famous promise: "I shall return"
  • April 9, 1942: ~75,000 US/Filipino troops surrender — largest in US history

  • Gen. Masaharu Homma tried & executed for war crimes; MacArthur returned Oct 1944

🏝️ Section 13: Pacific Theater: Tactics & Code

  • Pacific War = thousands of miles of open ocean, fights for tiny islands
  • Japanese military culture shaped how they fought — and refused to surrender

🗡️ Bushido (Japanese Military Code)

  • Bushido = "way of the warrior" — old samurai code adopted by modern military
  • Core values: loyalty, honor, courage, self-sacrifice
  • Surrender = permanent disgrace; death preferred over capture
  • Explains both fight-to-the-last-man defense and brutal POW treatment

✈️ Kamikaze ("Divine Wind")

  • Kamikaze = "divine wind" (named after 13th-century typhoon vs. Mongol fleet)
  • Suicide pilots crashed explosive-loaded planes into Allied ships
  • First major use Oct 1944, Leyte Gulf; heaviest at Battle of Okinawa (1945)
  • ~3,800 pilots died; mostly teenage, barely-trained pilots

🏝️ Island Hopping / Leapfrogging

  • Island hopping = bypass strongest Japanese islands, take weaker ones for bases
  • Bypassed islands cut off from supply & "withered on the vine"
  • Led by Adm. Nimitz (Navy) and Gen. MacArthur (Army)
  • Key islands: Guadalcanal, Tarawa, Saipan, Iwo Jima, Okinawa

❄️ Section 14: Battle of Stalingrad

  • Summer 1942: Hitler targeted Stalingrad on the Volga River in the Soviet Union
  • Wanted it for industry, Caucasus oil fields route, and propaganda (named after Stalin)
  • Brutal urban combat — house by house, room by room; Soviets "hugged" enemy to block bombing
  • Nov 1942: Soviets launched Operation Uranus, encircling the German Sixth Army
  • Hitler refused retreat; trapped Germans starved and froze through winter
  • Feb 2, 1943: Field Marshal Friedrich Paulus surrendered with ~91,000 men
  • ~2 million casualties total — bloodiest battle in history; turning point on Eastern Front

🪖 Section 15: D-Day

  • June 6, 1944 — Allies invade Normandy, France (code name Operation Overlord)
  • Largest amphibious invasion in history — ~156,000 troops crossed the English Channel
  • 5 beaches: Utah, Omaha, Gold, Juno, Sword — Omaha was bloodiest (American sector)
  • Supreme Allied Commander: US General Dwight D. Eisenhower (later 34th President)
  • ~4,400 Allies died on D-Day; Paris liberated by August 1944
  • Opened the "second front" — caught Germany in a vise (Soviets east, Allies west)
  • Called the "beginning of the end" for Nazi Germany

⚛️ Section 18: The Manhattan Project

  • 1939 — Albert Einstein letter warned FDR that Nazi Germany might build an atomic bomb
  • Top-secret US program (with UK & Canada) launched 1942 to build the atomic bomb first
  • Code-named the Manhattan Project; ran by Gen. Leslie Groves (military) & J. Robert Oppenheimer (science)

  • Bombs dropped on Hiroshima (Aug 6) & Nagasaki (Aug 9), ending WW2 and starting the nuclear arms race
