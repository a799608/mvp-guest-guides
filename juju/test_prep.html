🎲 Section 1: Probability — Letter Tiles

Letter tiles in a bag: A H T E A · W C A U E · T S E A C
15 total tiles. Counts: A×4, H×1, T×2, E×3, W×1, C×2, U×1, S×1.
Vowels (A, E, U): 8 total. Consonants (H, T, W, C, S): 7 total.
Replacing = put the tile back (total stays 15). Keeping = tile NOT put back (total becomes 14, and that letter has one less).
Write probabilities as fractions like 2/15.

What is the probability that Zack draws the letter T?

💡 How many Ts are in the bag? Divide by total tiles.

What is the probability that Zack draws the letter A?

💡 Count the As. Divide by total tiles.

What is the probability that Zack draws a vowel (A, E, or U)?

💡 Add up A count, E count, and U count. Divide by 15.

Is Zack more likely to draw a vowel or a consonant?

💡 Count each group and compare which is bigger.

What is the probability of drawing A, REPLACING it, then drawing T?

💡 Multiply P(A) by P(T). With replacing, total stays the same both draws.

Probability of drawing C, KEEPING it, then drawing E? Simplify.

💡 Multiply P(C) by P(E). With keeping, total drops by 1 for the second draw.

Probability of drawing S, REPLACING it, then drawing U?

💡 How many Ss in the bag? How many Us? Multiply those probabilities.

Probability of drawing a letter that is NOT a vowel, KEEPING it, then drawing A? Simplify.

💡 P(not vowel) first. Then P(A) from a bag of 14 tiles.

⚖️ Section 2: Inequality Word Problems

For each problem: 1) name the variable, 2) write the inequality, 3) solve.
Use >= for "at least" / "no less than", <= for "at most" / "no more than", > for "more than", < for "less than".

1) Your baseball team has a goal to collect at least 160 blankets. They brought 42 on Monday and 65 on Wednesday. How many must they donate Friday to make or exceed the goal?

What is the variable?

💡 Friday is the unknown count.

Write the inequality.

💡 Add Monday + Wednesday + Friday. Compare to 160.

Solve: fewest Friday blankets needed.

💡 Subtract what they already have from 160.

2) Your brother has $2000 saved for vacation. His airplane ticket is $637. How much can he spend on everything else?

What is the variable?

💡 The unknown dollar amount.

Write the inequality.

💡 Spending + ticket has to fit in $2000.

Solve: most he can spend on everything else (in dollars).

💡 Start with $2000, subtract the ticket.

3) You get a $15 weekly allowance. Bowling league costs $6.50 each week. You want to save at least $5 each week. How much do you have left to spend?

What is the variable?

💡 What is LEFT after bowling and savings.

Write the inequality.

💡 Bowling + savings + leftover spending must fit in $15.

Solve: most you can spend each week (in dollars).

💡 Subtract bowling and savings from the allowance.

4) Carla charges $45/hr for babysitting. Kathy charges a $15 travel fee plus $40/hr. After how many hours is Carla more expensive than Kathy?

What is the variable?

💡 Hours is what changes.

Write the inequality (Carla > Kathy).

💡 Carla = 45h. Kathy = 15 + 40h.

Solve for hours (write as an inequality).

💡 Subtract 40h from both sides, then divide by 5.

5) The science club charges $4.50 per car at their car wash. How many cars to earn at least $300?

What is the variable?

💡 Number of cars washed.

Write the inequality.

💡 $4.50 times c cars must hit at least $300.

Solve: fewest whole cars they must wash.

💡 Divide 300 by 4.50. Round so the total still hits $300.

6) You earn $6.15 per hour at a dry cleaner. You spent $30 on gas. How many hours to profit at least $100?

What is the variable?

💡 Hours worked.

Write the inequality (profit = earnings − gas).

💡 Earnings minus gas must be at least $100.

Solve: fewest whole hours.

💡 Add 30 to 100 first, then divide by 6.15.

7) The school band sells calendars for $0.40 each. Their goal is to earn more than $327. Fewest calendars to reach the goal?

What is the variable?

💡 Number of calendars sold.

Write the inequality (use >, not ≥).

💡 Use > because the goal is MORE than $327 (not equal).

Solve: fewest whole calendars.

💡 Divide 327 by 0.40. Round so the total beats $327.

8) An elevator can safely lift at most 4400 lbs. Each concrete block weighs 41 lbs. Maximum blocks?

What is the variable?

💡 Number of concrete blocks.

Write the inequality.

💡 41 times b blocks must fit under 4400 lbs.

Solve: maximum whole blocks.

💡 Divide 4400 by 41. Round so total stays under 4400.

📊 Section 3: Theoretical & Experimental Probability

Use survey data to find probabilities (as fractions) and expected counts for a bigger group.
Expected count = (count in survey / survey size) times new group size.

Survey of 24 students — favorite hobby:
Singing 1 · Hanging with friends 3 · Building things 1 · Bike riding 2 · T.V. 3 · Computer 3 · Roller skating 3 · Sports 8   (total 24)

P(roller skating)? Simplify.

💡 How many chose roller skating? Divide by 24, then simplify.

If 200 students were surveyed, how many expected to pick roller skating?

💡 Take the probability you found, multiply by 200.

If 60 students were surveyed, how many expected to pick bike riding?

💡 How many chose bike riding? Multiply that fraction by 60.

If 150 students were surveyed, how many expected to pick sports?

💡 How many chose sports? Multiply that fraction by 150.

Survey of 104 students — favorite winter activity:
Building a snowman 14 · Snowboarding / skiing 69 · Sledding 21   (total 104)

P(building a snowman)? Write as a fraction, simplify.

💡 How many chose snowman? Divide by 104, then simplify.

If 500 people responded, how many expected to pick sledding? Round to nearest whole person.

💡 How many chose sledding? Multiply that fraction by 500, then round.

Survey of 100 students at a 2000-student high school — plans after graduation:
Community college 24 · 4-year college 43 · Year off before college 12 · Trade school 15 · Do not plan to go 6

P(takes a year off before college)? Simplify.

💡 How many took a year off? Divide by 100, then simplify.

P(trade school or community college)?

💡 Add the trade-school count and community-college count. Divide by 100.

P(not 4-year college)?

💡 Subtract the 4-year count from 100. Divide by 100.

P(no college or year off)? Simplify.

💡 Add no-college count and year-off count. Divide by 100, then simplify.

How many of the 2000 students expected to attend a 4-year college?

💡 What fraction picked 4-year? Multiply that by 2000.

How many of the 2000 students expected to take a year off?

💡 What fraction picked year off? Multiply by 2000.

Hannah’s wildflower bloom counts:
Queen Anne’s Lace 5 · Coneflower 8 · Wild Cosmos 11 · Oxeye Daisy 6   (total 30)

P(Wild Cosmos)?

💡 How many Wild Cosmos? Divide by 30.

P(Oxeye Daisy or Coneflower)? Simplify.

💡 Add Oxeye and Coneflower counts. Divide by 30, then simplify.

If Hannah plants 135 seeds, how many coneflowers expected to bloom?

💡 What fraction were coneflowers? Multiply that by 135.
